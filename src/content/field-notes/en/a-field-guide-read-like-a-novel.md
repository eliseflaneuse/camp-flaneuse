Nobody warns you that a bird guide has plot, politics, and at least one villain.

I have been reading the field guide — the one from under the passenger seat, finally liberated from under the cat — the wrong way: cover to cover, like a novel. You are supposed to consult a field guide. You are not supposed to *read* it. But consultation only tells you what you just saw. Reading tells you everything you have been failing to see.

Read in order, a field guide is full of drama. There are birds described as "unmistakable," which is the author daring you. There are birds described as "easily confused with the previous species," which is the author apologizing in advance. There is one warbler whose entire entry amounts to *good luck*, and I respect the honesty.

The villain, in my edition, is a decoy species that appears on four different pages just to be ruled out. It is nobody and it is everywhere. Every mystery needs one.

The plot, meanwhile, is the migration maps: little arrows crossing continents, drawn as calmly as bus routes. Thousands of kilometers, twice a year, on the strength of instinct and body fat — footnoted like it's nothing.

Books read differently in a parked vehicle. That is the whole finding of this note. The guide will go back under the seat, and the cat will go back on the guide, and both arrangements are correct.
