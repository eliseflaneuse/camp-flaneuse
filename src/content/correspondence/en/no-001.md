**Delivered by the Correspondence Desk**
**The Soft Footsteps Society**
**Camp Flaneuse**

Dear you,

This letter has reached you because something in your way of moving through the world has been noticed.

Please do not be alarmed. The Society's methods of observation are neither advanced nor especially organized. They consist mainly of windows, notebooks, long walks, and Professor Emberroot becoming distracted in public places.

Still, certain signs are difficult to miss.

Perhaps you have stopped in the middle of a path because a bird was doing something peculiar.

Perhaps you have carried home a stone, feather, leaf, shell, ticket, button, or other object with no practical value and no intention of explaining yourself.

Perhaps you have looked at an abandoned house and wondered what the rooms still remember.

Perhaps you have felt, more than once, that the world contains meanings it refuses to explain directly.

You may have been accused of distraction.

We consider it a qualification.

The world has not become less miraculous. We have only been trained to hurry past the evidence.

The Soft Footsteps Society exists to collect some of that evidence before it disappears.

A drawing made badly but attentively.

A story that has survived by changing shape.

A field note written before the light moves.

A photograph of something no one else thought to photograph.

A question that has already survived several inadequate answers.

A life arranged according to rules its owner chose deliberately.

The world produces these things constantly. It is also very good at losing them.

This is where we come in.

Professor Emberroot finds things.

The Archivist labels them, repairs their dates, and asks why they were delivered covered in mud.

The cats contribute hair.

The camp continues.

Attention is not passive here.

To notice what the world teaches us to ignore is already a form of refusal. To remain curious when certainty would be easier is another. We preserve nothing by making it still. Things are allowed to remain alive in the archive: unfinished, inconvenient, changing shape, and fully capable of answering back.

You are receiving this letter because we suspect you may already be doing similar work.

Perhaps without a title.

Perhaps without realizing it was work.

Perhaps while everyone around you was walking slightly too fast.

You have not been chosen because you are exceptional in some grand and measurable way.

You have been noticed because you already behave like one of us.

You are therefore invited to enter the camp.

No arrival date is required.

No uniform will be provided.

There are no examinations, although you may occasionally be asked to look closely at something everyone else has agreed is unimportant.

You do not need to travel anywhere. Camp Flaneuse has never been strict about geography. It has appeared beside rivers, inside notebooks, beneath museum ceilings, at the edge of forests, and in the ten quiet minutes someone spends watching a beetle cross a path.

Membership begins wherever you happen to notice something and decide not to look away.

Before we continue, there is a note attached to your file.

---

### Professor Emberroot to the Archivist

Archivist,

I believe we have found another one.

The signs are familiar: unnecessary attention to birds, a tendency to wander without adequate justification, and a suspicious resistance to accepting the world exactly as it has been presented.

Please prepare the first enclosure.

Also, have you seen my green notebook?

—E.

---

### Reply from the Archivist

Professor,

You left the green notebook inside the blue notebook.

Again.

The first enclosure is prepared, although I continue to object to the phrase "unnecessary attention."

No attention is unnecessary.

You know this and persist only to irritate me.

I have added the new correspondent to the provisional records.

Please refrain from promising them a badge. We do not have badges.

—The Archivist

---

### Professor Emberroot to the Archivist

Archivist,

We could make badges.

—E.

---

Your first enclosure is a story.

It concerns a deer, a truck, and the consequences of preserving only the parts of a creature that are convenient to look at.

The Archivist wishes it recorded that this is not a particularly cheerful beginning.

Professor Emberroot wishes it recorded that it is a story about taking something back.

Both statements have been permitted to remain in the file.

There are many ways to walk softly.

You may write.

You may draw.

You may learn the name of a bird and then spend three weeks telling everyone who will listen.

You may sit close enough that another body does not have to be afraid alone.

You may leave water outside during hot weather.

You may rescue a story from the version that made it obedient.

You may make a place for old myths to live without pinning them down until they stop moving.

You may simply notice.

Mary Oliver wrote that paying attention is our endless and proper work.

The Society agrees, although we have added several unnecessary shelves and a correspondence department.

This is our first letter.

Others may follow carrying field notes, drawings, stories, maps, practical knowledge, misplaced creatures, unfinished questions, reports from roads that do not appear important until someone walks them slowly, and occasional disagreements concerning the correct classification of clouds.

You are not required to agree with everything contained here.

Agreement is not the purpose.

Attention is.

Should you wish to receive further correspondence, leave your address with the Desk.

**[Join the correspondence](/contact/)**

Letters will arrive when there is something worth carrying.

We cannot promise regularity.

We can promise attention.

Come in when you are ready.

With soft footsteps,

**The Archivist**
*for the Correspondence Desk*
*The Soft Footsteps Society*
*Camp Flaneuse*
