Every camp has a story it tells about itself. This is ours. It is told the way it was received: at dusk, in a low voice, with the fire down to embers and at least one cat pretending not to listen.

They say there is a deer that walks the edges of places where people are trying to change their lives.

Not the middles of those places. The edges. The tree line behind the last parking spot. The far bank of the river. The exact seam where the light gives up and the woods take over the accounting. If you are living an ordinary life, correctly, with all your papers in order, you will never see it. The deer has no business with you, and it is a very businesslike animal.

But if you are between things — between one life and the next, between the person you were told to be and the person you keep almost becoming — then some evening, in some unremarkable place, you may look up and find it already there. It does not arrive. It is simply present, the way a thought is present before you have words for it.

It will be carrying something.

Nobody agrees on what. The accounts contradict each other with the confidence of all true accounts. A ribbon. A key. A small bell that does not ring. A folded paper that is certainly a letter, a map, a diagram of a machine for staying whole. The only detail every version shares is this: whatever the deer carries, you will recognize it, quietly and completely, the way you recognize your own name spoken in a crowd — because it used to be yours.

Something you set down in an old life and did not mean to leave behind. Something you traded for being manageable. A particular laugh, maybe. A way of standing in a doorway. An intention.

There is one rule, and the rule is the whole story: you must not walk toward the deer. You may stop. You may look. You may let the kettle go cold. But the moment you try to close the distance — to take the thing back by force, to make the encounter *useful* — the deer steps into the trees and the woods close behind it like water, and what it carries goes back into the dark for another few years.

You are only allowed to notice. Noticing, at this camp, is considered a complete action.

Now — and this part I can vouch for less than I would like — someone we know stood once on the bank of a slow green river at dusk, in a country she had no particular reason to be in, and the tree line opened, and the deer was there. Present. Businesslike. Carrying something she recognized so completely that she did not dare name it, not even inside her own head, in case naming it counted as reaching.

They looked at each other for longer than these things usually last. The river went on doing its little spell. Then the deer turned — unhurried, the way an archivist turns after confirming a document exists and is safe — and walked back into the trees, taking whatever it carried with it.

She has not stopped thinking about it. Neither has the camp. Because here is the thing the older versions of the story insist on, the detail that keeps everyone warm and slightly unsettled:

The deer is not a thief. The deer is an archivist.

Nothing it carries is lost. It is *kept* — filed somewhere just past the tree line, in whatever cabinet the woods use, waiting with the patience of paper. What it keeps, and whether it can be asked to give something back, and what it might cost to ask —

that is the business of Part II.

The fire is low. The cats have opinions. That is enough for tonight.
